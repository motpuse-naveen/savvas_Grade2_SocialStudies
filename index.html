In This Text Set

This text set includes three informational texts, a video, and a collection of photos. All deal with aspects of making, buying, or selling products.

How Does Clothing Get to the Store? is the anchor text for this set. Depending on the needs of your class, you may choose to assign How Does Clothing Get to the Store? for independent reading, or you may choose to read it aloud with the class. Beginning with the anchor text will ground students in concepts and academic vocabulary that are key to discussing the Big Question. As students read the other texts in the set, they will compare their new understandings, as well as the structure and features of the text, with the anchor text.

After students read the anchor text, you may choose to assign specific texts or encourage students to choose the texts that appeal to them the most. Use the chart below to see where specific text features, text structures, and content topics appear across the texts in this set.

    D2.Eco.5.K-2. Identify prices of products in a local market.

How Does Clothing Get to the Store?

by Kim Haynes
Informational Text - Word Count: 336   Lexile: 500

Instructional Focus

  • Social Studies Standard: Identify prices of products in a local market.

  • Content Objective: Students will understand why products have different prices.

  • Language Objective: Students will describe features of a bike and explain their effects on the price.

  • Text Structure Objective: Students will understand how a compare and contrast structure shows how things are alike or different.

  • Text Feature Objective: Students will learn how section heads help them locate information in a text.

Summary

This informational text explores different bikes, from a simple model to a more expensive electric bike. Photos and details compare the bikes’ features and prices so readers can decide which bike is best.

Before Reading

Big Question
Remind students of the Big Question: Why do things cost what they do? Ask: How do you think Which Bike Would You Pick? will help you think about the Big Question? Ask students what they think a bike might cost, and why.

Preview Vocabulary

Have students find and read sentences in the text that have the words cost, price, and expensive. Discuss the meaning of each word with the class. Ask: If you see an ad for a toy, does it tell you the price? Do you think a bike is expensive? What do you think a bike would cost?

Oral Vocabulary
Some of the vocabulary in Which Bike Would You Pick? might be new or challenging for students. During class discussions, use these new terms repeatedly to help students become familiar with them. For example: A producer is a person or company that makes something, such as a bike. What is an example of a producer? How might a person design a bike?

K-W-L CHART

Share a K-W-L graphic organizer. Explain that this organizer can help students to think about vocabulary words they already know, words they want to know, and words they learn about from the text. Help students practice using a word that they already know, such as cost. Say: Do you already know the word cost ? Where would you add that word? What about the word produce? If you are still learning that word, where would you add that word to the chart?

[a three-column chart with columns labeled Know, Want, and Learn. List the following words in each column: cost, price
expensive
produce, design]

During Reading

ELEMENTS OF INFORMATIONAL TEXT

Text Structure Explain that this text has a compare and contrast structure. Say: In this text, the writer explains how some things are alike and different. Have students preview pages x-x of the text. Say: Both of these photos show bikes. How are they alike? How are they different?

Text Features Direct students to p. x. Point out that some words are bigger and bolder than others. Say: These big, bold words are headings. They tell what each part of the text is about. Point to the heading “A Bike for Rocks and Trails.” Ask: What would you expect to read about in this section?

Track the Texts: Have students use the Making, Buying, and Selling Things: Track the Texts graphic organizer. As they read, have them fill in the Which Bike Should You Pick? column with the name of the text structure, and any text features they noticed, including headings.

After Reading

VOCABULARY

After students have read Which Bike Should You Pick?, have them review their completed K-W-L charts and share them with a partner. Ask: Are there any more words that you would like to add?

Display these images from Which Bike Should You Pick? to help students think about vocabulary words they may have learned from the text, such as produce and cost.

DISCUSS ACROSS TEXTS

Encourage students to make connections between Which Bike Should You Pick? and other texts they have read on related subjects.

  • Which Bike Should You Pick? explain what different bikes cost. What are some other facts about how much things cost?
  • This text describes how people might make a decision about what to buy. What else have you read about buying something?
  • Think back to what you learned about how much it costs to make a shirt in How Does Clothing Get to the Store? How does that relate to what you learned in Which Bike Should You Pick?

Track the Texts: After the discussion, have students return to the Making, Buying, and Selling Things: Track the Texts graphic organizer. In the Which Bike Should You Pick? column, have them fill in the Big Ideas row with something they learned about where prices come from.

DISCUSS THE BIG QUESTION

Lead a class discussion around this text’s Big Question: Why do things cost what they do? As students share their ideas, draw out more information from them with follow-up questions such as, “Can you say more about that?” or “What are some examples of that?”

  • Why do some bikes cost more than others?
  • Which of the bikes would you pick? Why would you choose it? How would the price help you make a decision?

WRITE ABOUT THE BIG QUESTION

Have students design and draw a bike they would want, choosing parts or features based on the bikes in the text. Say: Think about the parts of each bike you read about. Which parts would you want your bike to have? Ask students to write two to three sentences describing their bike and telling how much they think the bike might cost.

Make a Lemonade Stand

by Anton Carpio
Informational Text - Word Count: 300   Lexile: 500

Instructional Focus

  • Social Studies Standard: Identify prices of products in a local market.

  • Content Objective: Students will understand the process of selling a product.

  • Language Objective: Students will describe steps in the process of making and selling lemonade.

  • Text Structure Objective: Students will understand how a procedural text structure can help readers learn how to complete a task.

  • Text Feature Objective: Students will understand how headings make it easier to locate information within a text.

Summary

Did you ever wonder how to start your own business? This text explains the steps for planning and running a lemonade stand.

Before Reading

Big Question
Remind students of the Big Question: Why do things cost what they do? Ask: How do you think Make a Lemonade Stand will relate to the Big Question? This text is about opening your own business and selling a product. How do you think selling a product would help you understand what it costs?

Preview Vocabulary

Have students find and read sentences in the text that have the words business, customers, and price. Discuss the meaning of each word with the class. Ask: Would you like to start your own business? If you go shopping with your family, are you customers? Prices tell what something costs. How do you find prices when you shop?

Oral Vocabulary
Much of the vocabulary in Make Your Own Lemonade Stand might be familiar to most students, but other texts in this set include words that might be new or challenging. During class discussions, use these new terms repeatedly to help students become familiar with them. For example: When you design something, you decide what it will look like. How would you design a lemonade stand?

K-W-L CHART

Share a Know-Want-Learn graphic organizer to help students think about words they already know, words they want to know more about, and words they learn as they read. Model adding a familiar word to the chart. Say: Do you already know the word price? Let’s add that word under Know. What about the word customer? If you are still learning that word, where would you add it to the chart? Guide students to add to the chart with other words related to starting a business or making a product. As they read, they can add new words under Learn. A completed K-W-L chart might look like this:

[a three-column chart with columns labeled Know, Want, and Learn. List the following words in each column:price, sell, buy, work business, goods customers]

During Reading

Elements Of Informational Text

Text Structure Tell students that this text uses a sequential structure. Say: A sequential text tells what happens in a sequence or process. It explains what happens first, next, and last. Have students read the title. Say: What process do you think this text will describe?

Display a T-chart graphic organizer. At the top of the left column, write “Sequential Structure.” Work with the class to fill in the column with what they know about this structure, and how it helps them understand the writer’s ideas. For example: “tells what happens in order”

Text Features Have students preview pages x-x in the text. Say: Do you notice some words that are bigger and bolder than the others? Those are called headings. You can use headings to help you find information inside a text. The heading tells you about the information that comes after it. Point to the bold heading 1. First, get some help. Say: It looks like this is the first step for making a lemonade stand. The text under this heading will explain how to get help. Have students look ahead to page x of the text. Ask: Which heading should I look under if I want to know how much my lemonade should cost? (5. Decide on a price.)

Return to the T-chart. At the top of the right column write “Headings.” Work with the class to fill in the column with examples of how headings help readers understand the text. For example: “help readers find information in a text”

After Reading

VOCABULARY

After students have read Make a Lemonade Stand, have them review their completed K-W-L charts and compare their charts with a partner’s. Ask: Are there any more words that you would like to add?

Display these images from Make a Lemonade Stand to help students think about vocabulary words they may have learned from the text, such as business, cost, and persuade.

DISCUSS ACROSS TEXTS

Encourage students to make connections between Make a Lemonade Stand and other texts they have read on related subjects.

  • Make a Lemonade Stand explains how to start a business. What else have you read about kids who have their own business?
  • This text tells how to make lemonade. What other texts describe how people make something?
  • Think back to what you read in How Does Clothing Get to the Store? How is selling clothes like selling lemonade? How is it different?

Track the Texts: After the discussion, have students return to the Making, Buying, and Selling Things: Track the Texts graphic organizer. Have them fill in the Make a Lemonade Stand row with something they learned about starting a business.

DISCUSS THE BIG QUESTION

Lead a class discussion around this text’s Big Question: Why do things cost what they do? As students share their ideas, draw out more information from them with follow-up questions such as, “Can you say more about that?” or “What are some examples of that?”

  • Do you think 50 cents is a good price for lemonade? Why or why not?
  • What would you need if you wanted to start a lemonade stand?
  • What would you need to think about before deciding on a price?

WRITE ABOUT THE BIG QUESTION

Have students create a comic strip about how to open a lemonade stand. The comic strip should include drawings and labels to describe some of the steps in the process. Remind students of steps they can include, such as buying ingredients, making the lemonade, and setting a price.

Farmer’s Market: Meet Me at Third and Fairfax

Word Count: N/A   Lexile: N/A
 

Instructional Focus

  • Social Studies Standard: Identify prices of products in a local market.

  • Content Objective: Students will understand how a farmer’s market is a different experience for both sellers and buyers.

  • Language Objective: Students will describe the experience of shopping for goods at a farmer’s market.

  • Text Structure Objective: Students will understand how a chronological structure shows events in the order in which they happened.

  • Text Feature Objective: Students will understand how videos use images and narration to communicate information.

Summary

This video provides a guided tour of a farmer’s market, following a young boy who spends the day there.

Before Reading

BIG QUESTION

Remind students of the Big Question: Why do things cost what they do? Ask: How do you think Farmers Market: Meet Me at Third and Fairfax will help you think about the Big Question? Have you seen the prices at a farmers market? What might affect or change those prices?

PREVIEW VOCABULARY

Discuss the words merchant, need, and want. Ask: What do merchants do at a market? When you go to a market, what are some things you can find that you need? What are some things that you want?

ORAL VOCABULARY

Much of the vocabulary in Farmers Market: Meet Me at Third and Fairfax might be familiar to most students, but other texts in this set include words that might be new or challenging. During class discussions, use these new terms repeatedly to help students become familiar with them. For example: A producer is someone who makes or grows something. Who are the producers of the products at a farmers market?

K-W-L CHART

Share a K-W-L graphic organizer. Explain that this chart can help students to think about vocabulary words they already know, words they want to know, and words they have learned from this reading. Help students to practice using a word that your students will already know, such as toy. Say: Do you already know the word need? Which column would you put that word in? What about the word merchant? If you are still learning that word, what column would you put that in? Have students create their own KWL chart on a sheet of paper.

[a three-column chart with columns labeled Know, Want, and Learn. List the following words in each column: need, want merchant produce]

During Reading

Elements of Informational Text

Text Structure Explain that Meet Me at Third and Fairfax has a chronological structure. Say: This video shows events in the order in which they happened. The video follows a boy who spends the day at the farmer’s market. Have students watch the first 10 seconds of the video. Ask: What is the first thing that happens in the video? (Possible response: The boy invites the viewer to come to the farmer’s market with him.) If you were going to make a video about your day today, what would be the first thing that you would show?

Display a T-chart graphic organizer. At the top of the left column, write “Chronological Structure.” Work with the class to fill in the column with what they know about this structure, and how it helps them understand the writer’s ideas. For example: “tells what happens in order”

Text Features Explain that the video uses both images and audio to take people on a tour of the market. Have students continue to watch the next 10 seconds of the video (from 00:10 to 0:20). Ask: Is that the boy talking in this part of the video? This video has a narrator, or someone telling what happens. You might have read stories that have a narrator. Narrators can also be helpful in some videos. Direct students to timecode 00:40 and have them watch 10-20 seconds. Ask: What is the narrator telling you? How does it help you understand the video?

Return to the T-chart. At the top of the right column write “Narration.” Work with the class to fill in the column with examples of how the narrator helps readers understand the text. For example: “helps make a topic clearer”

After Reading

VOCABULARY

After students have read Farmers Market: Meet Me at Third and Fairfax, have them review their completed K-W-L charts. Ask: Are there any more words that you would like to add?

DISCUSS ACROSS TEXTS

Encourage students to make connections between Farmers Market: Meet Me at Third and Fairfax and other texts they have read on related subjects.

  • What texts have you read about farmers markets or other places to shop?
  • In the Farmers Market video, you learned about farmers who grow crops that they sell. What texts have you read about farmers or other people who grow or make things?
  • Think about the shirt you read about in How Does Clothing Get to the Store? Do you think the shirt would be sold at a farmers market? Why or why not?

Track the Texts: After the discussion, have students return to the Making, Buying, and Selling Things: Track the Texts graphic organizer. Have them fill in the Farmer’s Market: Meet Me at Third and Fairfax row with what they learned about markets.

DISCUSS THE BIG QUESTION

Lead a class discussion around this text’s Big Question: Why do things cost what they do? As students share their ideas, draw out more information from them with follow-up questions such as, “Can you say more about that?” or “What are some examples of that?”

  • How would a shopper find out how much something costs at the farmer’s market?
  • Who works at a farmer’s market? What kind of work do they do? How do they decide how much the food costs?

WRITE ABOUT THE BIG QUESTION

Have students write a brief story (3-4 sentences) about a real or imagined trip to the farmer’s market. Their story should include specific details about what they see, smell, hear, or taste. It should be told in chronological order and should explain what they bought and how much it cost.

Shops and Markets Around the World

by Sloane Jackson
Informational Text - Word Count: 400   Lexile: 570

Instructional Focus

  • Social Studies Standard: Identify prices of products in a local market.

  • Content Objective: Students will understand how merchants display goods and sell them to customers.

  • Language Objective: Students will compare and contrast different places to shop around the world.

  • Text Structure Objective: Students will understand how a compare-and-contrast structure shows how things are similar or different.

  • Text Feature Objective: Students will understand how photographs help them understand the ideas in a text.

Summary

This informational text takes readers on a journey to shops and markets in different countries. Colorful photos show how people buy and sell products around the world.

Before Reading

Big Question

Remind students of the Big Question: Why do things cost what they do? Ask: How do you think Shops and Markets Around the World will help you think about the Big Question? What do you think things cost at the shop or market? How do the shop owners decide on prices?

Preview Vocabulary

Have students find and read sentences in the text that have the words market, sellers, goods, and product. Discuss the meaning of each word with the class. Ask: Have you ever been to a market? Who were the sellers at the market? What goods did they sell? What product did you want to buy?

Oral Vocabulary

Shops and Markets Around the World includes some words that might be new or challenging to students. During class discussions, use these new terms repeatedly to help students become familiar with them. For example: If you owned a toy store, how would you display or show the toys? What would persuade you to visit a shop, or make you want to go there?

K-W-L CHART

Share a K-W-L graphic organizer. Explain that it can help students think about new vocabulary as well as words they already know. Help students to practice using a familiar word, such as shop. Say: Do you already know the word shop? Let’s put that word under Know. What about goods? If you are still learning that word, where would you add it to the chart? Before they read, have students fill in the Know column with words about shops and markets that they already know. They can add vocabulary words they wish to know more about under Want. As they read, ask students to add any new words to the Learn column. A completed KWL chart might look like this:

[a three-column chart with columns labeled Know, Want, and Learn. List the following words in each column: shop, seller, market goods product, display, persuade]

During Reading

ELEMENTS OF INFORMATIONAL TEXT

Text Structure Explain that Shops and Markets Around the World has a compare and contrast structure. Say: Sometimes, writers explain how things are the same and different. Direct students to p. 11 of the text. Point out the word both in the caption. Tell students that some words, such as both and like, show that the author is comparing two things, or telling how they are the same. Other words, such as differ and but, show that the author is contrasting two things by telling how they are different.

Display a T-chart graphic organizer. At the top of the left column, write “Compare-and-Contrast Structure.” Work with the class to fill in the column with what they know about this structure, and how it helps them understand the writer’s ideas. For example: “explains how things are the same and different”

Text Features Say: This text has a lot of photos in it. Photos can show how things are the same or different. Point out the photo of a market on p. 4. Ask: What does this photo show? What can you buy at this market? Then, direct students’ attention to the photo of a grocery store on p. 6. Ask: How is this shop similar to the market in the first photo? How is it different?

Return to the T-chart. At the top of the right column write “Photographs.” Work with the class to fill in the column with examples of how the photos help readers understand the text. For example: “help show similarities and differences”

After Reading

VOCABULARY

After students have read Shops and Markets Around the World, have them review their completed K-W-L charts and share them with a partner. Ask: Are there any more words that you would like to add?

Display these images from Shops and Markets Around the World to help students think about vocabulary words they may have learned from the text, such as goods, display, product, and customer.

[images from the book for the teacher to project: photo from screen 8 (bookstore in Spain) photo from screen 6 (U.S. supermarket) photo from screen 14 (mall in Turkey)]

DISCUSS ACROSS TEXTS

Encourage students to make connections between Shops and Markets Around the World and other texts they have read on related subjects.

  • In Shops and Markets Around the World, we learned that there are many different places to buy and sell things. What else have you read about buying and selling products?
  • In this text, some merchants grow or make the food they sell. What are some facts you know about farming or making food?
  • Think about what you read about making and selling clothes in How Does Clothing Get to the Store? Look at the photos in this text. How do these products get to the market?

Track the Texts: After the discussion, have students return to the Making, Buying, and Selling Things: Track the Texts graphic organizer. Have them fill in the Shops and Markets Around the World row with something they learned about buying and selling products.

DISCUSS THE BIG QUESTION

Lead a class discussion around this text’s Big Question: Why do things cost what they do? As students share their ideas, draw out more information with follow-up questions such as, “Can you tell me more about that?” or “What are some examples of that?”

  • Think about the products in a market. How do you think sellers decide what they will cost?
  • Why might one product cost more than another product?
  • Why might a customer decide to shop at a market? How can prices help people decide where to shop?

WRITE ABOUT THE BIG QUESTION

Have students imagine opening their own shop. Ask them to write a few sentences describing the shop. Say: Think about the places you read about. Where would your shop be? What would you sell, and how much would it cost? Ask students if they would make their own products or sell things that someone else made.

Wrap It Up

Now that the class has read some or all of the texts in this set, it is time to pull together all the information they have learned from their reading in order to explore and share their evolving understanding of the Big Question.

Vocabulary Round-Up

Have students review all the vocabulary K-W-L charts they completed for these texts. Ask students to look for words that might have started out in the L (learn) or W (want to know) column that later moved to the K (know) column. For example, if they wanted to know more about the word expensive when reading the anchor text, they might have known this word by the time they saw it in Which Bike Would You Pick?

Have students identify any words they still have listed as “want to know.” Invite volunteers to share their “want to know” words aloud, and ask other volunteers if they can explain or define the words. Model using reference materials to look up unknown words.

Discuss the Text Set's Big Question

Guide a class discussion around the Big Question: Why do things cost what they do? Encourage students to bring in knowledge they have learned from all the texts they have read in the text set, using as many of the vocabulary words as they can.

  • You’ve read a lot of books about making, buying, and selling products. What did you already know about this topic before you began reading this text set?
  • What did you learn about why things cost what they do?
  • What else would you like to learn about making, buying, and selling products?
  • Which texts were the most helpful in answering the Big Question? Why?

Culminating Project

Have students apply what they’ve learned from the text set to demonstrate and enhance their understanding.

Create a Product Plan

Have students write and draw a step-by-step plan for making and selling a product at a school craft fair. Ask them to think about the materials they need and how they will design the product. Remind them of the steps involved in selling the product, including setting a price. If you wish, you can extend the activity by organizing a class Market Day in which students make products they designed and “sell” them using play currency.

Discussion Tips

An important part of building a vibrant classroom culture is creating an atmosphere in which all students feel empowered to participate in powerful classroom conversation. You may have noticed that your students who are naturally extroverted often participate freely in a whole-class discussion, while those who are more introverted or, perhaps, who come from cultures that respect different classroom norms, may need encouragement to share. You can use sentence prompts like these to invite all students into the conversation:

“That’s so interesting … please tell me more!”

“Can someone explain that in a different way?”

“What are some examples of that?”

“Can someone add on to that idea?”

Sharing and discussing ideas will often involve agreeing and/or disagreeing with what others say. As students listen carefully and respond thoughtfully to their classmates’ ideas, encourage them to express themselves in respectful and constructive ways. You may wish to post these sentence starters prominently, so students can refer to them often:

“I agree, and I also think fill in the blank line__________ .”

“I mostly agree, but fill in the blank line__________ .”

“I like what you just said because fill in the blank line__________ .”

“I respectfully disagree because fill in the blank line__________ .”
